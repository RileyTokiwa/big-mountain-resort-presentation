Big Mountain Resort Presentation

 Problem Identification

Context: Finding the most effective means of lowering overhead costs or increasing revenues in order to sustain the a new chair lift adding an additional cost Y/Y of $1,540,000. 

Criteria for success: The above increase in overhead must be counterbalanced by an increase in ticket prices or a reduction in operating costs equal to or exceeding the cost for maintenance of the new lift. 

Constraints: The ticket prices must be reasonably priced in order to avoid lower customer turnover. Lowered operating costs from retiring features may also cause lower customer turnover. 

Desired Features and Ticket Price Collinearity 

Features like: the number of fast quads, the total number of chairs, and the total vertical drop were the most important when determining ticket price. 

Big Mountain was several standard deviations ahead of the competition in regards to all the important features which was the reason that the model considered it undervalued despite being in the highest percentile for ticket prices modeled.

Price Increase as per Modeled Ticket Valuation

1. Considering that the new chair lift will create a new 150ft vertical drop as well as add a chair lift, within the context of the model this would raise the price valuation for tickets by $1.99.
2. The new chair lift would be sustained by an increase in ticket price of $0.88 to cover the overhead Y/Y. 
3. This model was made assuming an average customer turnout of 350,000 per season paying for 5 days each. 

Conclusion/Recommendation

​The price model has predicted that the increase in ticket valuation from the new chair lift will be enough to offset the costs. An increase in ticket prices of $1.99 is sustained with the current model and is the recommended action.
